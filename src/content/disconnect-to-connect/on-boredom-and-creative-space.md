When was the last time you were genuinely bored?

Not distracted, not busy, not resting — actually bored, with nothing to do and nothing to fill the gap with.

For most people reading this, the answer is: not recently. Possibly years.

## What boredom actually is

Boredom is the mind looking for a problem to solve. It is restlessness with nowhere to go — and that restlessness, if you let it run, tends to find something genuinely interesting.

The best ideas I've had in the last decade arrived during a commute with no headphones, a meal eaten alone without a book, a long drive without podcasts. Moments I would now instinctively fill.

## The cost of constant stimulation

Every podcast, every scroll, every notification is a small bid for your attention. Individually harmless. Collectively, they leave no gaps.

Ideas need gaps. Connections between disparate things need the mind to wander without a destination. We've optimised that wandering out of our days and called it productivity.

## An experiment worth trying

For one week, try commuting without audio. No podcast, no music, no audiobook. Just the journey.

It will feel uncomfortable for the first few days. Then it will start to feel like something else — a kind of thinking you'd forgotten you were capable of.

## The paradox

The more we fill our time, the less we seem to have. The mind that is never quiet rarely produces anything quiet and good.

Boredom is not a problem to be solved. It is a condition to be occasionally allowed.
